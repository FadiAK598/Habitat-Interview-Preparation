What the role involves

You'll sit within the trading team and provide around-the-clock coverage for Habitat's portfolio of battery storage assets. Day-to-day this means monitoring live assets against expected dispatch, responding to anomalies, ensuring trader instructions are executed correctly, confirming positions with counterparties, and producing performance and incident reports.

What they're really screening for

  • Operational composure — can you stay accurate and calm when something goes wrong at 2am?
  • Attention to detail — trade confirmation errors have direct P&L consequences
  • Decisiveness — explicitly listed in the JD; they want someone who acts, not waits
  • Market awareness — you don't need to be a trader, but you need to understand the revenue context
  • Excel competency — a hard requirement; expect a test
  • Genuine interest in energy storage — generic "passion for renewables" won't cut it

Energy Markets — Technical
Questions on GB electricity markets, battery storage economics, and market structures. Glassdoor confirms these are asked directly, including questions on FFR, Dynamic Containment, and market modelling.
Revenue Stacking & Markets
M01 Explain how a battery storage asset generates revenue across multiple markets. ⌄
Model Answer
"Battery storage assets can stack revenue across three main streams simultaneously. In the wholesale market, they charge when electricity is cheap (typically overnight or when renewables flood the grid) and discharge when prices are high — capturing the spread. In ancillary services (such as Dynamic Containment, Dynamic Regulation, or Firm Frequency Response), assets earn availability payments by holding capacity ready to respond to grid frequency deviations — often within 0.5–1 second. In the Balancing Mechanism, National Grid ESO can accept a battery's bids and offers in real time to correct supply/demand imbalances, generating additional income. Habitat's value proposition is optimising how and when an asset participates across all three simultaneously — maximising returns while managing degradation."
Key insight: The three streams interact — committing to a frequency service means that capacity isn't available for arbitrage. Optimisation is about finding the right mix moment by moment.

M02 What is the Balancing Mechanism and how does a battery participate in it? ⌄
Model Answer
"The Balancing Mechanism (BM) is the real-time tool used by NESO to balance electricity supply and demand across GB. Generators and flexible assets submit Physical Notifications (PNs) — their planned import/export profile for each half-hour settlement period — along with bid and offer prices for deviating from that profile. If NESO needs more generation (or less demand), it accepts bids and offers accordingly, which is called a Bid-Offer Acceptance (BOA). For a battery, this means it can offer to discharge (an 'offer') or charge (a 'bid') at a set price, and NESO may call on it in real time. Revenue comes from the accepted BOAs and can be significant during system stress events."
Operational relevance: As a Scheduling Analyst, you'd be responsible for ensuring PNs are submitted accurately and on time. Late or incorrect PNs can result in imbalance charges.
Revise in source documents
Elexon Beginner's Guide — pp. 2–3, 8–11 (Bids, Offers, BM, PNs) NESO Balancing Costs FAQ — "What is the BM?" (p. 3)
M03 What is Dynamic Containment and why is it particularly valuable for battery storage? ⌄
Model Answer
"Dynamic Containment (DC) is a post-fault frequency response service procured by NESO. It requires assets to respond within one second of a frequency deviation, delivering power proportionally to the size of the deviation — the response is dynamic rather than binary. It's split into DC High (responding to over-frequency) and DC Low (under-frequency). Batteries are ideally suited because they can respond in milliseconds, far faster than any thermal plant. NESO pays an availability payment per MW regardless of whether the asset is actually called upon, which makes DC a reliable, low-activity revenue stream — particularly attractive when wholesale spreads are thin."
Key term: DC operates at ±0.5 Hz from 50 Hz. It is a post-fault service — meaning it kicks in after a significant event (like a large generator tripping), not continuously like Dynamic Regulation.
M04 What is the difference between Firm Frequency Response (FFR) and Dynamic Containment? ⌄
Model Answer
"Both are frequency response services, but they differ in structure and timing. FFR (static): triggers at a fixed frequency threshold (typically 49.7 Hz), delivers a fixed power output within 30 seconds, and is sustained for 30 minutes. It is a simpler, threshold-based product procured via tender. Dynamic Containment: responds within one second, is proportional to the frequency deviation, and is a post-fault service designed for faster, finer grid stabilisation. DC has largely replaced FFR as the primary fast frequency response product in GB, as it's better suited to a grid with high renewable penetration where frequency events are faster and less predictable."
Note: NESO also procures Dynamic Regulation (DR) and Dynamic Moderation (DM) for continuous, pre-fault frequency management. DR is the most commonly stacked service alongside DC for batteries.
M05 What is a Physical Notification (PN) and why does it matter operationally? ⌄
Model Answer
"A Physical Notification is the declared import/export profile that a Balancing Mechanism Unit submits to NESO, specifying in MW what the unit plans to do in each settlement period. They must be submitted and updated within a gate closure window. Operationally, PNs are critical because they form the baseline against which NESO measures deviations. If an asset operates significantly differently from its PN without a corresponding BOA, it incurs Imbalance Settlement charges — which can be very expensive. The Scheduling Analyst role involves ensuring PNs reflect trader intentions accurately and are updated promptly when plans change."
Elexon note: PNs are submitted via Elexon's BMRS/EDL systems. Gate closure in GB is currently one hour before delivery — after which changes to PNs are more restricted.

M06 How does state of charge (SoC) affect a battery's ability to participate in ancillary services? ⌄
Model Answer
"State of charge is a fundamental operational constraint. For frequency services, the asset needs to be able to respond in both directions — charging if frequency is high, discharging if frequency is low. This means the SoC needs to be held within a range that allows headroom in both directions: too high and you can't absorb more energy (DC High fails); too low and you can't discharge (DC Low fails). For most ancillary services, operators target a mid-SoC band — often 40–60% depending on the duration and terms of the service. SoC management is therefore a continuous operational task, and unexpected drift (due to a market event or asset issue) needs to be corrected promptly."
Operational implication: Monitoring SoC is one of the most concrete live operations tasks you'd perform — it directly affects revenue capability and contract compliance.
Revise in source documents
CAISO 2024 Battery Report — §2.1 (SoC constraints, ancillary service state-of-charge constraints)
M07 What factors determine whether a battery should prioritise wholesale arbitrage vs ancillary services on a given day? ⌄
Model Answer
"It comes down to a comparison of expected revenues from each pathway, adjusted for risk and constraints. For wholesale arbitrage, the key input is the day-ahead price spread — the difference between peak and off-peak prices. Wide spreads (often driven by high renewable output at midday depressing prices) make arbitrage attractive. For ancillary services, the clearing prices from NESO tenders are known in advance, offering predictable availability payments. A battery optimiser like Habitat would weigh the risk-adjusted return of each: ancillary services offer certainty but limit flexibility, while arbitrage is more volatile but can be very lucrative during high-spread periods. Degradation costs also factor in — ancillary services often impose fewer charge/discharge cycles than arbitrage."
This is a trader-level question. At this stage of your career, demonstrating you understand the trade-off logic is sufficient — you don't need to have built the optimisation model yourself.
Revise in source documents
CAISO 2024 Battery Report — §2.4–2.5 (Energy bids, arbitrage patterns, ancillary service share) World Bank BESS Guidelines — §3.3, Fig. 9 (Revenue models: bulk energy shifting vs ancillary)
M08 What is the role of NESO and how does it interact with market participants like Habitat Energy? ⌄
Model Answer
"NESO — the National Energy System Operator, formerly National Grid ESO — is the independent body responsible for operating the GB electricity transmission system in real time. Its core job is to keep the grid balanced at 50 Hz at all times. It interacts with market participants like Habitat in several ways: procuring ancillary services (Dynamic Containment, DR, FFR etc.) via tenders in advance; accepting bids and offers in the Balancing Mechanism during gate closure; and issuing dispatch instructions (BOAs) when it needs an asset to deviate from its PN. Habitat, as an asset operator on behalf of clients, acts as the interface between NESO and the physical battery — ensuring the asset responds correctly to NESO instructions."
Recent change: NESO was formally separated from National Grid in October 2024, becoming an independent, government-owned body. Worth mentioning to show current awareness.

M09 What is a Bid-Offer Acceptance (BOA) and what happens operationally when one is issued? ⌄
Model Answer
"A Bid-Offer Acceptance is an instruction from NESO to a Balancing Mechanism participant to deviate from their Physical Notification. When NESO accepts a bid or offer, it issues a BOA specifying the volume and direction of the change required. Operationally, receiving a BOA means the asset must respond and adjust its output accordingly — for a battery, that means charging or discharging at the instructed MW level. Once the asset acts on the BOA, NESO calculates the deviation from the PN and settles accordingly. If we receive a BOA and the asset doesn't respond — due to a fault, communication issue, or SoC constraint — that non-delivery becomes an imbalance that needs immediate escalation and documentation."
Direct ops relevance: Tracking BOA receipt and confirming asset response is a real-time task in this role. A missed BOA is one of the most commercially significant operational failures.
Revise in source documents
NESO FAQ — "Once we accept a bid or offer, we issue Bid Offer Acceptances (BOAs)" (p. 4) Elexon Beginner's Guide — pp. 9–11 (Bid-Offer Acceptances, gate closure, settlement)
M10 What is imbalance settlement and why does it matter if a battery deviates from its PN without a BOA? ⌄
Model Answer
"Imbalance settlement is the process by which ELEXON calculates and prices the difference between what a BMU declared it would do (its PN) and what it actually did (its metered volume). If a battery charges or discharges outside its declared PN and without a corresponding BOA, that deviation is treated as an imbalance. The imbalance is priced at the System Buy Price or System Sell Price — which can be significantly worse than market rates, especially in stressed system conditions. This is why PN accuracy is commercially critical: the cost of an unexpected imbalance during a high-price period can be very large. As Scheduling Analyst, ensuring the PN reflects trader intentions and is updated promptly when plans change is one of the most important responsibilities."
From the Elexon guide: Settlement is reconciled on four occasions over 14 months, with increasingly accurate metered data. Errors found late are still financially corrected.

M11 What are the main BESS revenue streams and how do they differ commercially? ⌄
Model Answer
"There are three main categories. Bulk energy shifting (arbitrage): buying cheap and selling expensive — revenue depends entirely on price spreads and carries market risk. Network and system services (ancillary services): contracted availability payments from NESO (e.g. Dynamic Containment, STOR) — predictable, lower-risk income that doesn't require active dispatch most of the time. Capacity Market: annual payments for holding capacity available to dispatch during system stress. These three can be stacked, but they create competing constraints — committing capacity to a service limits how it can be used elsewhere. The World Bank BESS guidelines categorise projects by primary use case because the commercial structure of each is fundamentally different: energy shifting deals are price-risk driven, service contracts are performance-obligation driven."
Interview tip: The World Bank framework of three project types — bulk shifting, network services, hybrid — maps directly to how Habitat structures its optimisation decisions.
Revise in source documents
World Bank BESS Guidelines — §3.3, Tables 7–8, Fig. 9 (Revenue models by use case)
M12 How do batteries participate in frequency regulation and what operational constraints does this create? ⌄
Model Answer
"Batteries providing frequency regulation respond automatically to deviations from 50 Hz — discharging when frequency drops (under-frequency), charging when it rises (over-frequency). In CAISO and similar markets this is called regulation up/down; in GB it's Dynamic Containment or Dynamic Regulation. The key operational constraint is that the battery must maintain sufficient SoC headroom in both directions at all times during the service window. The CAISO 2024 report found batteries provided 84% of regulation up and down requirements — demonstrating how dominant storage has become in frequency markets. From an operations perspective, when a battery is providing regulation it cannot be freely dispatched for arbitrage, so the scheduler must actively track which assets are committed to which services at any given time."
Key figure to cite: CAISO data shows batteries provided 84% of frequency regulation in 2024 — strong evidence for why BESS and regulation services are inseparable.

M13 What is BSUoS and who ultimately pays for the cost of balancing? ⌄
Model Answer
"BSUoS — Balancing Services Use of System — is the charge that recovers the costs of operating the Balancing Mechanism. It's levied on generators and suppliers in proportion to their market share of generation and consumption in each settlement period. Generators recover it through their revenue streams; suppliers pass it through to consumers. Balancing costs contribute less than 5% of a household's electricity bill, but in aggregate they represent billions of pounds annually — and have increased significantly since 2018 due to gas price volatility and grid constraint costs. For a battery operator like Habitat, understanding BSUoS matters because it affects the competitive dynamics of the BM: if balancing costs are high, the spreads available from BOA revenue are larger."
Contextual awareness: NESO has forecast constraint costs alone could reach £3 billion/year by 2028. This is the commercial environment Habitat's assets operate in.

NESO FAQ — "Who pays for Balancing Costs?" + "How is my energy bill impacted?" (pp. 3–4)
M14 What is a Settlement Period and why does the half-hourly structure matter for battery dispatch? ⌄
Model Answer
"In GB, electricity trading and settlement is structured around 48 half-hour Settlement Periods per day — SP1 covers 00:00–00:30, through to SP48 (23:30–00:00). All contracted volumes, Physical Notifications, and metered deviations are calculated per settlement period. For battery dispatch, this half-hourly structure sets the minimum granularity at which positions need to be managed. A battery's PN must specify its planned MW output for each half-hour window. If the asset does something materially different within that window without a BOA, it creates an imbalance for that settlement period. This is why real-time monitoring needs to be continuous — an anomaly early in a settlement period needs to be caught and addressed before the end of that half-hour window to minimise imbalance exposure."

Scenario-based questions about monitoring, fault response, escalation, and shift handover. These are the day-to-day realities of the role.
Monitoring & Fault Response
O01 Walk me through how you would monitor a live battery asset and detect anomalies. ⌄
Model Answer
"I'd start each monitoring window by establishing the expected operating state: what market the asset is committed to, its scheduled dispatch profile, and its current SoC. From there, I'd watch for deviations across three dimensions: commercial (is the asset following its nominated position?), technical (is the telemetry behaving consistently — power output, SoC, temperature, inverter status?), and market (have any NESO instructions been issued that we haven't responded to?). An anomaly might be a BOA we haven't executed, an SoC drifting outside the expected range, or a communication gap in the telemetry feed. Each anomaly has a different response path — some I'd try to resolve directly, others I'd escalate immediately per the issue policy."
Interview framing: Even without direct BESS experience, showing a structured, three-dimensional approach to monitoring demonstrates operational maturity that interviewers recognise.
O02 An asset is not responding to a dispatch instruction. What do you do? ⌄
Model Answer
"First, I'd confirm the instruction was actually sent correctly and the timing was within the expected response window — rule out a system or comms issue on our side. Then I'd check the asset's telemetry: is it receiving the command? Is there a fault flag? Is the SoC at a constraint limit that would prevent the response? If I can't identify and resolve the issue within a short window, I escalate immediately — both to the technical team (asset fault) and to the trader (commercial implication). I'd document the timeline as I go: when the instruction was issued, when non-response was detected, what I checked, and what was escalated and to whom. NESO may also need to be notified if the asset is committed to a service and cannot deliver."
Key principle: In live operations, doing nothing while waiting for more information is itself a decision — and often the wrong one. Act, escalate, and document in parallel.
O03 How would you write an incident report after an asset failure? ⌄
Model Answer
"I'd structure it around five components: (1) Timeline — when was the issue first detected, by what means, and by whom; (2) Nature of the fault — what exactly failed and what symptoms were observed; (3) Commercial and operational impact — what dispatch was missed, what revenue was affected, were any contract obligations breached; (4) Actions taken — what was done in real time to mitigate, including escalations and their timestamps; (5) Root cause and follow-up — what caused the failure, any tech tickets raised, any process changes recommended. The writing should be factual and specific — no assumptions, no vague timings. The test is: could someone reading this six months later reconstruct exactly what happened?"
Why they ask this: Incident reporting is explicitly in the JD. A structured answer here signals both operational competence and communication maturity.
O04 What would you check first if an asset's state of charge was reading unexpectedly high or low? ⌄
Model Answer
"My first step is to determine whether the unexpected reading is real or a data artefact. I'd cross-reference multiple data sources: does the SCADA reading match the BMS output? Does the cumulative net energy flow since last known SoC align with the current reading? If there's a discrepancy between data sources, it's likely a sensor or communication issue rather than the asset actually being in that state. If the readings are consistent and the SoC is genuinely off, I'd look at what dispatch has occurred — was there an unplanned activation, an unauthorised instruction, or a fault that caused unexpected charging or discharging? I'd then assess whether the SoC deviation puts us at risk of breaching a service contract obligation and escalate if so."
Key insight: Always distinguish between a data problem and a real operational problem first — the response path is very different.
O05 How do you ensure a clean handover at the end of your shift? ⌄
Model Answer
"A clean handover has three parts. Status summary: what is each asset currently doing, what market is it in, and what is its current SoC? Open items: any issues that are unresolved, ongoing investigations, or pending escalations — with full context so the incoming operator doesn't start from scratch. Upcoming flags: any known events in the next few hours that the incoming shift should be aware of — NESO tenders, asset outage windows, maintenance schedules. I'd deliver this verbally in a structured handover call and leave a written log. The outgoing operator is responsible until the incoming one confirms they've received and understood the handover — not just when the clock says the shift ends."
Why it matters: Poor handovers are a leading cause of operational incidents in 24/7 environments. Showing you understand this is a mark of operational maturity.
O06 What's the difference between a curtailment event and an asset fault? How would you distinguish them? ⌄
Model Answer
"Curtailment is an instructed reduction in output — from NESO, the grid operator, or potentially from a constraint management instruction. The asset is operating correctly but has been told to reduce or stop. A fault is an unplanned deviation from expected operation due to a technical problem — hardware failure, inverter trip, communication loss, etc. To distinguish them: curtailment will typically appear as an instruction in the market systems (a BOA or constraint notice), and the asset's telemetry will show it responding to that instruction. A fault will show fault flags in the BMS or SCADA, and the deviation won't correspond to any instruction. Misidentifying one for the other can lead to the wrong response — escalating to NESO for a 'curtailment' that's actually a fault, or vice versa."
O07 How would you manage warranty condition tracking for a portfolio of battery assets? ⌄
Model Answer
"Battery warranties typically include conditions around cycle count, depth of discharge, temperature operating ranges, and sometimes minimum SoC thresholds. I'd manage this with a live tracking spreadsheet or database that records: the warranty conditions per asset, current cumulative metrics (cycles used, average DoD), projected run-rate versus warranty limits, and a flagging system for assets approaching boundaries. Any planned dispatch that would put an asset near a warranty limit should be flagged to the trading team before execution. I'd also ensure that any warranty-relevant events — like an overheat or deep discharge — are captured in the incident log immediately, even if resolved quickly, because they may be needed for a future warranty claim."
Direct JD link: "Ensure asset warranty conditions are tracked and managed effectively" is listed verbatim as a responsibility. They may ask this specifically.

D02 How would you build a performance monitoring report for a portfolio of battery assets? ⌄
Model Answer
"I'd start by defining the key metrics: availability (% of time each asset was operational and in-market), dispatch accuracy (actual vs. scheduled output in MWh), revenue captured vs. optimisation target, and any contract compliance metrics. I'd structure the workbook with a raw data tab, a calculations tab, and a summary dashboard. The dashboard would show at-a-glance status for each asset — RAG status against targets — with drilldown capability into the detail. I'd include trend charts over rolling periods (7-day, 30-day) so that degradation or recurring issues are visible over time, not just in the most recent period. Any cell that feeds a headline metric would be clearly labelled and auditable."
D03 Describe how you've used data analysis to support a decision or identify a problem. ⌄
Model Answer
"In a previous role, we noticed that a particular process was running consistently slower than target on certain days of the week. Rather than accepting this as random variation, I pulled the full dataset and filtered by day, operator, and input type. I found that the slowdowns clustered on Mondays and correlated with a specific upstream data feed that was being refreshed over the weekend. The root cause wasn't operational — it was a data pipeline latency issue that no one had thought to check. I presented the finding with charts showing the correlation and proposed a simple fix. The analysis took a few hours but saved the team recurring confusion. The lesson I took is: when something is consistently off, data usually tells you why — you just have to ask the right question of it."
D04 If you received a dataset of half-hourly actual dispatch vs. scheduled dispatch for a battery asset, how would you analyse it? ⌄
Model Answer
"I'd start by loading the data and checking for completeness — any missing periods, obvious outliers, or formatting issues. Then I'd calculate the deviation per settlement period (actual minus scheduled) and look at the distribution: are deviations random, or do they cluster at certain times of day, certain markets, or certain asset states? I'd calculate cumulative deviation over the period to understand the total magnitude. I'd flag periods where deviation exceeded a threshold (say ±5% or an absolute MW level) and investigate those specifically. If there's a pattern — e.g. consistent under-delivery in the morning peak — that points to a systematic issue (SoC management, dispatch logic, or comms lag) rather than noise. I'd present findings in a chart showing scheduled vs. actual over time, with deviation annotated."
This is essentially the job. Reconciling scheduled vs. actual dispatch is a core daily task. A strong answer here is a direct signal of operational readiness.

Motivation & Fit
Questions about your reasons for applying and your long-term direction. Generic answers here will cost you — be specific about Habitat's model and what makes this role commercially interesting.
F01 Why energy storage specifically? Why not a more traditional energy or operations role? ⌄
Model Answer
"Battery storage sits at the intersection of two things I find genuinely interesting: real-time operational complexity and commercial optimisation. A traditional generation or grid operations role might be procedurally rigorous, but the asset behaviour is relatively predictable. Storage is different — the optimal strategy changes by the hour based on price signals, frequency conditions, and SoC constraints. The role is intellectually active, not just procedurally correct. I also think this is where the most interesting energy infrastructure growth is happening right now — the GB storage market is expanding rapidly and the operational and market frameworks are still maturing. Getting deep expertise in this area now feels like a high-value career investment."
F02 Why Habitat Energy specifically? What do you know about how we differentiate ourselves? ⌄
Model Answer
"Habitat is not an asset owner — it's a technology and optimisation company that operates on behalf of clients. That distinction matters: the commercial model is built around algorithmic and machine-learning approaches to maximising returns across stacked revenue streams, rather than just managing a fixed dispatch schedule. You've recently expanded significantly in ERCOT — the UBS 730 MW partnership and the Birch Creek solar-plus-storage deal — which shows the model is scaling beyond GB. That's a more interesting operating context than a traditional utility, and the team structure brings together trading, data science, and engineering in a way that would expose me to a broad set of skills, not just one function."
Research matters here. The interviewers will be able to tell immediately whether you've read about the company or are giving a generic answer. Reference specific deals, markets, or the ML/algorithmic angle.
F03 How do you feel about shift rotations including nights and weekends? ⌄
Model Answer
"I've thought about this carefully and I'm genuinely comfortable with it — it's part of what makes this role operationally meaningful. Energy assets don't stop generating or responding to market signals on Friday evenings, and the most commercially significant events (grid stress, price spikes) often happen outside standard hours. I'd approach the shift pattern the same way I'd approach any operational responsibility: with structure, a consistent pre-shift routine, and discipline around rest. I wouldn't apply for this role if the shift element were a dealbreaker for me — I'd rather have a role where the responsibility is real."
F04 Where do you see this role taking your career in 2–3 years? ⌄
Model Answer
"My immediate goal is to develop deep operational competence in live battery asset management and the GB market structure — to become genuinely reliable and effective in the core role. Over 2–3 years, I'd want to progress toward either a more senior scheduling or operations position, or potentially toward the trading side as I develop better intuition for market dynamics. I'm also interested in the data and analysis dimension — there's clearly a lot of value that can be unlocked from operational data, and I'd like to contribute to that over time. I'm not looking to move quickly for the sake of it — I want to build a foundation that's actually useful."
Avoid: Saying you want to be a trader within 12 months. That can come across as using this role as a stepping stone, which may concern the interviewer.
